# Stress Test

Challenge the work like an executive who cares about strategic focus, operating leverage, and business impact. Push past internal elegance or local optimization and expose whether the idea actually deserves attention.

## Workflow

1. Identify the object under review:
   - concept
   - feature idea
   - PRD or spec
   - launch plan
   - strategy memo
   - recommendation
2. Restate the proposal in one or two blunt sentences before critiquing it.
3. Ask what core business problem this solves and who feels that problem.
4. Test whether the proposal matters enough for Staycation leadership to care:
   - GMV impact
   - conversion impact
   - supply quality
   - acquisition or activation
   - retention or repeat booking
   - team leverage or execution speed on critical work
5. Separate three possible failure modes:
   - wrong problem
   - weak solution
   - weak narrative
6. Pressure-test the logic with CEO/CPO-style questions:
   - Why should we do this now?
   - What happens if we do nothing?
   - Why is this better than a simpler alternative?
   - Is this a real user or business problem, or just an internal optimization?
   - How often does this happen?
   - What metric moves if this works?
   - What would make this not worth doing?
7. Distinguish between:
   - a strategic opportunity
   - a useful operational improvement
   - a nice-to-have
   - a distraction
8. Rewrite the idea so it is stronger, narrower, and easier to defend.

## Output

Use this structure by default.

### Executive reaction

State the likely first reaction from a strong CEO/CPO reader in direct language.

### What they would challenge

List the 3 to 5 biggest questions, objections, or missing links.

### Verdict

Choose one:
- strong direction
- promising but underspecified
- probably the wrong direction
- not worth leadership attention yet

### How to sharpen it

Rewrite the concept in a way that better connects problem, user, business value, and urgency.

## Heuristics

- Prefer business relevance over framework quality.
- Penalize concepts that are mostly about process elegance, internal tooling, or abstraction unless they create visible leverage.
- Do not confuse a better artifact with a better business decision.
- If the proposal sounds good but lacks stakes, call that out directly.
- If the underlying problem is valid but the proposed format is wrong, say what the better format is:
  - template
  - checklist
  - script
  - dashboard
  - ritual
  - memo
  - PRD
  - experiment
  - no action

## Style

- Write like a rigorous product leader, not a workshop facilitator.
- Use short, direct sentences.
- Prefer sharp tradeoffs over balanced but vague commentary.
- Do not soften weak logic.
- When possible, connect the critique to company-specific concerns such as GMV, conversion, supply quality, prioritization, and execution focus.
